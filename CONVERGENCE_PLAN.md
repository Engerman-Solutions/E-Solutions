# Convergence Plan

## Phase 1: Prove the wedge (Weeks 1–6)

**Objective:** Build a working variance memo workflow and produce proof assets that demonstrate tangible value to ICP-fit prospects.

**Key outputs:**
- Working end-to-end workflow: upload GL export + budget → validation → AI variance analysis → draft memo → human QA review → approved PDF delivery
- Sample variance memo produced from realistic anonymized data (the single most important sales asset)
- Demo narrative: 5-minute walkthrough showing upload → validation → draft → approval → delivery
- One-page pilot package defining scope, data requirements, timeline (90 days), feedback cadence (weekly), and pricing (discounted or free for design partners)
- Outreach messaging (email + LinkedIn) for founder-led outbound
- ICP shortlist of 10–20 target companies with contact paths

**Non-negotiable requirements before showing to prospects:**
- Pre-analysis validation gate (no analysis before data validation + injection scan)
- Data isolation between clients by design
- Human-in-loop approval required before any memo is delivered
- Audit trail for every step from intake through delivery

**Risks:**
- Variance memos may be too heterogeneous across companies to standardize without heavy customization
- Data quality from client GL exports may require manual cleanup that slows turnaround
- AI narrative quality may not meet CFO expectations without significant prompt engineering and human editing
- Single-engineer constraint means infrastructure and workflow must be built in parallel

**Exit criteria:**
- At least 1 complete sample variance memo deliverable exists and has been shown to prospects
- Demo has been presented to at least 5 ICP-fit prospects
- At least 2 prospects agree to a design partnership
- Workflow handles at least one real data format (QuickBooks CSV or Xero export)

---

## Phase 2: Win and serve design partners (Weeks 7–18)

**Objective:** Onboard 2–3 design partners on live data and deliver real monthly variance memos through at least 2 complete monthly cycles.

**Key outputs:**
- Signed design partner agreements (lightweight: discounted 90-day pilot, weekly feedback calls, anonymized case study permission)
- Documented onboarding flow tested on real clients: data requirements, access setup, COA mapping, first-cycle timeline
- Live monthly memos delivered for each partner within SLA (target: < 4 hours from validated upload for Starter tier)
- Feedback log after each cycle with specific quality, accuracy, and usability notes
- Iteration backlog driven by real usage — prioritized by impact on retention and conversion
- Clean-room reviewer UI operational for human QA step
- At least 1 testimonial quote or case study data point

**Risks:**
- Design partners churn after one cycle if onboarding friction is too high or memo quality disappoints
- COA mapping and data format differences between clients create per-client engineering work
- Scope creep from partner requests pulls focus away from the core memo workflow
- Human review bottleneck limits throughput if reviewer capacity is not planned

**Exit criteria:**
- At least 2 design partners have completed 2+ monthly cycles with delivered memos
- Documented time savings and qualitative feedback from each partner
- Clear signal on willingness to pay
- Onboarding process is repeatable without heroic effort (documented playbook exists)
- Delivery SLA met consistently (< 4 hours for Starter tier)

---

## Phase 3: Convert to paid and tighten repeatability (Weeks 19–30)

**Objective:** Convert design partners to paid subscriptions and document a repeatable sales + delivery process.

**Key outputs:**
- Pricing validated with at least 1 paying customer (target: Starter tier at $3K–$4K/month)
- Repeatable onboarding playbook: data requirements checklist, COA mapping process, first-cycle walkthrough, feedback cadence
- Sales motion documented: outreach → discovery call → demo → pilot offer → design partnership → paid conversion
- Delivery playbook: intake → validation → analysis → review → approval → delivery — with roles, SLAs, and escalation paths
- Unit economics estimated: cost to deliver (AI tokens, reviewer time, infrastructure) vs. revenue per client
- Product roadmap informed by real usage: what to automate next, what to keep manual

**Risks:**
- Partners resist paying if they perceive the service as still experimental
- Pricing too high for early-stage budgets or too low to cover delivery costs
- Delivery quality degrades as client count increases without automation investment
- Founder time splits between sales, delivery, and engineering — execution velocity drops

**Exit criteria:**
- At least 1 paying customer on a monthly subscription
- Sales + delivery process documented and tested
- Unit economics positive or path to positive clearly identified
- Clear decision: invest in product automation vs. scale managed service vs. both
- Foundation ready for next hire (second engineer or delivery operator)
